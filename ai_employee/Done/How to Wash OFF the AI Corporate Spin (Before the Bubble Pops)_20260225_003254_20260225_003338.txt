This edition shows you how to dodge the spin cycle and keep your career intact while the bubbles rise. 🧼 The AI Washing Edition 🧼 By Andrew Allen and Carla Dewing ⚡ Are You Pro or Anti AI? Wash This. AI didn’t break your job. Bad leadership did. Welcome to the era of AI washing: when companies say AI-first, mean cost-cutting, and expect you to smile while learning 17 new tools for the same pay. Everyone hates AI right now. Reddit’s mad. The media’s mad. Your coworkers are “not scared” but also deeply suspicious. Executives, meanwhile, are living their best AI lives. Cool. Normal. Very healthy! This edition is about seeing clearly and staying employable - in a workplace that’s getting louder, messier, and more AI-washed by the day. AI went from “future of work” to “reason your friend got laid off” in record time. Not because it failed - but because it got stapled to: Layoffs Bigger workloads ROI promises no one can explain Turns out people don’t hate automation. They hate being told it’s the future while their job slowly disappears. 3 Ways to Survive AI Washing (Right Now) ☑️ Ignore the nutty extremes If you’re either screaming “AI will replace us all” or smugly saying “it’s all hype” - congrats, you’re not helping your career. The winners test AI in the grey zone while everyone else rage-tweets about it. ☑️ Become the AI boundary setter AI is great at repetition. Awful at judgment. Survivors are the ones who say: “AI stops here - and here’s why.” When no one sets limits, workers absorb the fallout. Don’t be that worker. ☑️ Make your work measurable before it disappears AI makes work faster - and your effort invisible. Smart people expose the delta: what AI did, what they decided, and what risks never happened because of good design. Speed without credit is a trap. ✅ Do This Now Write down where AI helps - and where it absolutely doesn’t Track what would’ve taken weeks without AI Stop sounding like everyone else in performance reviews AI panic is the new workplace sport. But if you're not careful, you'll get spin-cycled by someone else's nonsense. Forget being pro-AI or anti-AI. Shivam Gupta, Senior AI Innovation Specialist at IgniteTech answered: “The shift happened when I stopped thinking about ‘prompt engineering’ and started thinking about ‘context engineering.’ I now run every prompt through the intern test: if a smart but new intern wouldn’t know what I want, the model won’t either.” People who are good at delegating work to humans make natural context engineers. They already know how to give clear instructions and provide the right background. The skill transfers directly. The era of psychological tricks is over. You don't need to say "pretty please" or threaten the model with made up consequences. You just need to communicate clearly, the same way you would with any capable collaborator who knows nothing about your situation until you tell them.
